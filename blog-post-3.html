How FreshMart Surry Hills increased weekly revenue by 35% in 3 months

A
Anika Roy ·May 10, 2026 ·7 min read
🏪

When Raj Krishnamurthy, owner of FreshMart Surry Hills, joined Boota in November 2024, he was cautious. He had tried a delivery platform once before and found it brought more headaches than revenue. Boot delivery was different, he said, because "the customer still comes to me."

Three months later, his weekly revenue from Boota orders had increased by 35%. Here is what happened.

Month 1: Learning the rhythm

FreshMart's first week on Boota brought 47 orders — more than their entire previous month of phone pre-orders. The staff found the tablet app straightforward, though the first few days involved a learning curve around the packing checklist feature.

"The app is actually very simple," Raj told us. "My casuals picked it up within one shift. The hardest part was reminding them to mark items as ready, not just pack them and walk away."

Average order value in the first month: $67.40. Their typical in-store basket average at the time: $38.20. The difference comes from planned shopping — customers who browse an app before shopping buy more deliberately and spend more.

Month 2: Finding the peak slots

By month two, Raj could see in his admin dashboard which slots were most popular: Tuesday evenings and Saturday mornings accounted for 62% of orders. He adjusted staffing to match, scheduling one dedicated Boota packer during those windows.

"Before, I was overstaffed on Sunday afternoons and understaffed on Tuesday evenings. Now I know exactly when to roster people."

He also noticed that the most popular category was not what he expected: fresh produce, not pantry staples. Customers trusted Boota specifically for fruit and vegetables — items they would normally want to pick themselves. The substitution workflow (where staff notify customers of out-of-stock items and offer alternatives) built that trust.

Month 3: The compounding effect

By month three, something interesting was happening. Boota customers were becoming weekly regulars in a way that in-store customers rarely did. The app made it trivially easy to repeat a previous order, and customers were doing exactly that.

"I have customers I have never met who order from me every week," Raj said. "They do not even know what I look like. They just trust that the bags will be good, and they keep coming back."

At the end of month three, Raj's weekly Boota revenue was 35% above his baseline. His total store revenue was up 18% — Boota was not cannibalising walk-in trade; it was genuinely incremental.

What Raj would tell other store owners

"Do it. But invest in your packing workflow first. Get a dedicated shelf for Boota orders. Make sure your staff know exactly where everything is. The technology is simple — the operations are where you win or lose."
